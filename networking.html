Networking & Making Connections

Why Networking and Connections Matter

Making connections isn't just for social butterflies — it's vital for academic, professional, and personal growth. Effective communication helps you stand out and make a lasting impression, while networking opens doors to opportunities you might not even know exist!

UMSI students are future-focused: tomorrow's competitive Data Analysts, distinguished UI Designers, and skilled Librarians. But before you can truly excel in these fields, there's a universal foundation to build — outstanding networking skills.

No matter your chosen path in the information sciences, your technical talents alone won't carry you over the finish line; your ability to connect with others, share ideas clearly, and cultivate meaningful professional relationships is just as crucial. Networking isn't only for job hunting — it's how you find mentors, discover hidden opportunities, and collaborate on innovative projects that set you apart from the crowd.

How to Start Networking (Even If You're Shy!)

  • Attend Events: Go to campus meetups, workshops, or department gatherings.
  • Use Online Platforms: Connect with peers and professionals on LinkedIn, Slack, or U-M’s own tools like Maizey and Go Blue.
  • Find Common Ground: Start conversations about classes, shared interests, or campus news — pizza is almost always a safe topic.
  • Ask for Advice: People enjoy sharing their experiences. "What led you to your major/job?" is a great icebreaker.

Networking FAQ

  • When should I start networking?
    Right now! Seriously, it’s never too early. Building relationships during your studies gives you a head start when you need advice, recommendations, or job leads.
  • I’m introverted or shy—how can I network comfortably?
    Start small: join student organizations, attend low-pressure events, or connect with classmates online. Remember, networking is just building relationships with others—you don’t have to be the loudest in the room.
  • How do I follow up after meeting someone?
    Send a short, friendly email or LinkedIn message (within a day or two) thanking them for their time and mentioning something you discussed. Don’t ask for a job right away; build the relationship first!
  • What if I face rejection or an awkward moment?
    It happens to everyone! Don’t take it personally. Stay professional, thank the person for their time, and move on. Each experience builds confidence for the next one.
